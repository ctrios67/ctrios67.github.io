NYC Jobs Analysis

Courtesy of the following Data: NYC Job Listings Dataset

To view this on the tableau site: Click here!


Quick Note: While I thought it was cool to embed the User Story here, it might be more practical to open it in another tab while reading my analysis.


This contains 4 user stories created in Tableau, using job posting data, that tells the user the current number of job openings per agency, lowest salary offered by an agency, highest salary offered by an agency, and my own analysis to which agency is struggling to fill openings.


1.) Job Openings per Agency Worksheet:

Here we can see that the Department of Health/Mental Hygiene has the most jobs available. I just summed the # positions and sorted accordingly.


A note on the following 2 worksheets:

The data separates position pay by 3 dimensions, Annually, Hourly, and Daily. This needs to be taken care of, so I thought of 2 things. I can create 3 visualizations converting each to abide by Annual, and display them on a dashboard, but if you’re a business user, you want everything simplistic and fast. This answers the business question, but is not a practical implementation.

So I went with my second idea, create assumptions based on the data, create new calculated fields converting the different pay types into annual, and display them at once.

Assumptions:

  1. Daily pay works 5 days a week on average.
  2. Since hourly ranges from 35-40 based on the data, on average they work 37.5 hours.

The code pictured below shows how I implemented these assumptions into a new calculated field.

IF [Salary Frequency]="Daily"
THEN ([Salary Range From]*5)*52
ELSEIF [Salary Frequency]="Hourly"
THEN ([Salary Range From]*37.5)*52
ELSEIF [Salary Frequency]="Annual"
THEN [Salary Range From]
END

Notice the last is an ELSEIF, this is necessary because otherwise you’ll be conflicting with the hourly and daily again if it was just ELSE. A similar logic is implemented for Highest Paying, but switching [Salary Range From] for [Salary Range To].

2.) Lowest Paying Agency Worksheet:

Here we see that technically, the Consumer Affairs agency has a job offering the lowest pay, but if you look at the data and find this entry, the job description asks for 5+ years of experience. I highly doubt a person with 5 years of experience will take a job for free.

With that in mind, the agency with the lowest paying job is the Department of Design & Construction.

3.) Highest Paying Agency Worksheet:

Here the pay needs to be converted like before and a similar logic needs to be implemented for a new calculated field. No anomaly this time though, and we clearly see the Highest Paying agency is the Department of Mental Health/Hygiene. A medical field to no surpise.

4.) Hardest Jobs to Fill:

This is my personal analysis. I created another custom field called “Years Posted.” To make this one I took the date the job was posted, the date a job was supposed to be posted until, and if that end date was null, I made it the process by date, since it was in January of 2017 and seemed like a nice basis. I subtracted [Post Until] from [Posting Date] while handling nulls of [Post Until], and having a distinct count as the result was hard to interpret because they are datetimes, giving me results between 1 and 6 which made sense, and would make sense to a user.

With this, I colorized the departments by their oldest job postings, red signifies older, green signifies more recent. The size of the agency is determined by the # of positions, and hovering over the agency shows their lowest salary available.

My argument is, the agencies with the most jobs open AND the oldest job postings tend to offer the lowest salaries. This visualization exemplifies this analysis by my annotations. As lowest salaries get higher, job positions are significantly lower, showing they are not having trouble filling positions. The verdict is still out though on job postings where the salary is low and positions are low and the years posted is recent. One would measure this going further to see if this point stands true.
